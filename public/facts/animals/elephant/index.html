About Elephants

Elephants are the largest land animals alive today, with African bush elephants reaching 4 metres at the shoulder and weighing up to 7 tonnes. There are three recognised species — African bush, African forest, and Asian — and their evolutionary lineage stretches back 60 million years, encompassing mammoths, mastodons, and dozens of extinct relatives. The trunk alone contains over 40,000 individual muscle units, making it one of the most versatile appendages in the animal kingdom: capable of uprooting trees, picking up a single blade of grass, or spraying water with precision.

Elephant cognition is extraordinary by any measure. They are one of a small number of animals that recognise themselves in mirrors — a benchmark test of self-awareness shared with great apes, dolphins, and magpies. They mourn their dead, returning repeatedly to the bones of deceased family members and gently touching the skulls with their trunks. Matriarchs accumulate knowledge across decades, guiding herds to water sources and safe corridors that younger elephants have never encountered — a form of cultural memory that makes elder females the keystone of herd survival during drought.

Elephants communicate through a remarkably diverse repertoire. In addition to trumpeting and rumbling, they produce infrasound — vibrations below the threshold of human hearing — that travels through both air and ground at frequencies that other elephants can detect through their feet from kilometres away. This seismic communication allows herds to coordinate movements, signal alarm, and maintain contact across landscapes where visual or audible signals would be blocked by terrain.
