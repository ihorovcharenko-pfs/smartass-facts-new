Is the Five-Second Rule Real? What Food Safety Science Says

By SmartAss Facts Team · Last updated 2026-05-25 · More myths debunked →

⚡ Quick Answer

False. Bacteria begin transferring to food the moment it contacts a contaminated surface — not after five seconds. A 2016 Rutgers University study testing the five-second rule across different foods and surfaces found bacterial transfer on contact, with moisture being the dominant variable. Wet foods (watermelon, bread) picked up bacteria far faster than dry foods (gummy candy). Time matters, but it is not a safety threshold.

🔑 Key Takeaway

"Food is safe to eat if picked up within five seconds of falling on the floor." is a myth. The scientific consensus is clear, the primary sources are documented above, and the claim does not survive scrutiny. Read on for the full evidence.

✗ The Myth

"Food is safe to eat if picked up within five seconds of falling on the floor."

✓ The Reality

Bacterial transfer begins on contact, not after five seconds. The amount of bacteria transferred depends primarily on moisture content of the food and the surface type — time is a much smaller factor.

The five-second rule is one of those beliefs that almost everyone knows to be scientifically dubious yet repeats anyway, usually to justify eating something dropped on the kitchen floor. The claim that a brief floor contact is safe has a reassuring simplicity — but it is not how bacterial transfer works. Bacteria do not wait politely for five seconds before migrating. Contact is what matters, and the dominant variables are how wet the food is, how contaminated the surface is, and the texture of both surfaces. Time plays a role — longer contact allows more transfer — but there is no safe threshold below which food remains bacteria-free.

The Rutgers Study: Testing the Five-Second Rule

In 2016, food scientist Donald Schaffner and colleagues at Rutgers University published the most rigorous investigation of the five-second rule to date in the journal Applied and Environmental Microbiology. The team tested four surfaces (tile, carpet, wood, stainless steel), four foods (watermelon, bread, bread with butter, gummy candy), four contact times (1, 5, 30, and 300 seconds), and used Enterobacter aerogenes — a non-pathogenic surrogate for Salmonella — applied to the surfaces. Key findings: bacteria transferred in every single trial, including at 1-second contact. Watermelon (moist food, large surface area) picked up 96–99% of available bacteria within 1 second. Gummy candy transferred the fewest bacteria due to its low moisture and small contact area. Longer contact time did increase transfer somewhat, but the five-second cutoff showed no particular significance.

The Physics of Bacterial Transfer

Bacterial transfer from a surface to food depends on three primary factors: the moisture content of the food (wet foods transfer bacteria far more efficiently because water carries bacteria away from the surface), the type of surface (carpet transfers fewer bacteria than tile due to reduced surface contact area), and the amount of bacteria present on the surface. Time plays a secondary role — more contact time allows more transfer — but the relationship is not linear and there is no point at which food is clean. The "five-second rule" implies a threshold that does not exist in microbiology. The fundamental mechanism is physical contact and moisture-mediated transfer, not a timer.

Floor Contamination in Context

How dangerous dropped food actually is depends entirely on what's on the floor. Most household floors contain relatively low levels of harmful pathogens, and the immune system handles low-level exposures. Restaurant kitchen floors may contain more Salmonella or Campylobacter; bathroom floors more E. coli; hospital floors various pathogens. Eating dropped food from a kitchen floor at home is unlikely to cause illness in a healthy adult most of the time — not because of a five-second rule, but because floors are not uniformly contaminated with harmful organisms. This is very different from the food being clean.

The Verdict

The five-second rule has no scientific validity. Bacteria transfer on contact; time is a secondary variable. Whether eating dropped food will make you sick depends primarily on what was on that specific section of floor — which you have no way of knowing. The Rutgers researchers concluded simply: "The safest rule is: when in doubt, throw it out." In practice, the risk from a single dropped piece of food is usually low for healthy people — but this is about overall floor contamination levels, not a five-second grace period.

Frequently Asked Questions

Is the five-second rule real?

No. Bacteria transfer to food on contact — not after five seconds. A 2016 Rutgers study found bacterial transfer in every trial, including at 1-second contact. Time matters (more contact = more bacteria), but there is no threshold below which dropped food is free of bacteria from the floor.

What factors most affect how much bacteria food picks up off the floor?

The three main factors are: moisture content of the food (wet foods like watermelon transfer bacteria almost instantly; dry foods like crackers transfer far less); surface type (tile and steel transfer more bacteria than carpet); and how contaminated the specific area of floor is. Time is a secondary factor.

Is eating food off the floor actually dangerous?

For healthy adults, eating food dropped briefly on a typical household floor usually poses low risk — not because of a five-second rule, but because household floors are not heavily contaminated with dangerous pathogens most of the time. The risk is higher on floors in food-service areas, bathrooms, hospitals, or anywhere pathogens are more likely to be present. Immunocompromised individuals face higher risk.

Who invented the five-second rule?

The origin is unclear. A popular attribution to Genghis Khan (supposedly saying food could be on the ground for hours if he called it) is apocryphal. The rule appears in American popular culture at least from the 1970s and was first formally "tested" (and found invalid) by a high school student, Jillian Clarke, during a 2003 internship at the University of Illinois — she won an Ig Nobel Prize for the work.
