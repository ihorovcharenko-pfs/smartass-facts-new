Is the Tongue the Strongest Muscle in the Human Body?

By SmartAss Facts Team · Last updated 2026-05-25 · More myths debunked →

⚡ Quick Answer

False as stated. The tongue is remarkably strong relative to its size, but "strongest" is undefined. By absolute force: the masseter (jaw) at 200+ kg. By total work over a lifetime: the heart. By moving the body: the gluteus maximus. The tongue wins no absolute strength record.

🔑 Key Takeaway

"The tongue is the strongest muscle in the human body." is a myth. The scientific consensus is clear, the primary sources are documented above, and the claim does not survive scrutiny. Read on for the full evidence.

✗ The Myth

"The tongue is the strongest muscle in the human body."

✓ The Reality

"Strongest" is ambiguous. The masseter generates the highest bite force; the heart does the most cumulative work; the gluteus maximus is largest and most powerful for locomotion. The tongue is strong for its size but holds no absolute strength record.

This claim appears in children's encyclopaedias, school textbooks, and internet lists of fun facts with such frequency that it has the feel of established science. It is not. The tongue is indeed a remarkable muscle — it operates continuously, moves food in three dimensions during chewing and swallowing, and shapes speech sounds with precision. But calling it the "strongest muscle" requires a definition of "strongest" that no one using the claim actually provides, and by any standard definition, the tongue loses.

Measuring Muscle Strength: Multiple Standards

Muscle strength can be defined in multiple legitimate ways. Absolute force is the maximum force a muscle can generate in a single contraction. Power is force multiplied by velocity. Endurance is the ability to sustain contractions over time. Total work is the cumulative energy output over a lifetime. The muscle with the highest strength by any measure depends entirely on which measure you use. The "strongest muscle" claim collapses the moment you ask what metric is being applied.

The Strongest by Each Measure

By absolute force, the masseter — the jaw muscle used for chewing — is typically considered the strongest relative to its size, and generates bite forces of 70–170 kg in normal adults, with documented extremes exceeding 440 kg. By total work performed over a lifetime, the heart wins unambiguously: it contracts roughly 100,000 times per day, every day, for decades, pumping approximately 2,000 gallons of blood daily. Over a 70-year life, the heart does more cumulative mechanical work than any other muscle. By sheer mass and power for locomotion, the gluteus maximus is the largest muscle in the body and generates the most force for activities like climbing stairs, jumping, and running.

What the Tongue Actually Is

The tongue is not a single muscle but a structure made of eight interlacing muscles — four intrinsic (which change the tongue's shape) and four extrinsic (which move it within the mouth). This multi-muscle architecture gives the tongue extraordinary three-dimensional mobility that allows it to perform the complex coordinated movements required for speech, swallowing, and taste. The tongue is also one of the few muscles in the body with constant motor activity — it operates around the clock. It is impressive by these measures. But generating maximum force is not what the tongue is built for, and it does not compete with the masseter on bite strength or the heart on endurance.

The Verdict

The tongue is the strongest muscle in the body only if you define "strongest" in a way that specifically favours the tongue — and no one applying the claim ever does. By the standard measures of strength (maximum force, power, mass, cumulative work), the masseter, heart, and gluteus maximus all exceed it. The tongue is a remarkable muscle; it just does not hold the record claimed for it.

Frequently Asked Questions

Is the tongue the strongest muscle in the human body?

No — "strongest" is undefined. By maximum force, the masseter (jaw) generates more. By total work over a lifetime, the heart. By mass and power for movement, the gluteus maximus. The tongue is strong for its size but holds no absolute strength record.

What is the strongest muscle in the human body?

It depends on the measure. Masseter (jaw): highest force relative to size, bite forces of 70–170+ kg. Heart: most total cumulative work over a lifetime. Gluteus maximus: largest muscle, most powerful for locomotion. All are legitimate candidates depending on definition.

How many muscles does the tongue have?

Eight. Four intrinsic muscles (superior and inferior longitudinal, vertical, transverse) change the tongue's shape — making it wider, flatter, longer or shorter. Four extrinsic muscles (genioglossus, hyoglossus, styloglossus, palatoglossus) anchor it to surrounding structures and move it within the mouth.

Is the tongue the hardest working muscle?

The heart performs the most total mechanical work over a lifetime by a significant margin. The tongue works continuously throughout waking hours and during swallowing in sleep, but its continuous low-load activity does not compare with the heart's decades of non-stop, high-load pumping.

Can you actually tie a knot in a cherry stem with your tongue?

Yes — with practice. The tongue's remarkable three-dimensional mobility (courtesy of its eight interlacing muscles) allows skilled individuals to manipulate the stem into a knot. It is a display of dexterity and fine motor coordination, not strength per se.
