Does Sugar Make Kids Hyperactive? What Science Actually Shows

By SmartAss Facts Team · Last updated 2026-05-23 · More myths debunked →

⚡ Quick Answer

False. More than a dozen double-blind, placebo-controlled trials have found no link between sugar consumption and increased activity, attention problems, or behaviour changes in children — even in those diagnosed with ADHD or described as 'sugar-sensitive' by their parents.

🔑 Key Takeaway

"Sugar Makes Kids Hyperactive" is a myth. The scientific consensus is clear, the primary sources are documented above, and the exact origin of the false belief can be traced. Read on for the full evidence.

✗ The Myth

"Sugar Makes Kids Hyperactive"

✓ The Reality

False.

Every birthday party seems to confirm it: children eat cake and sweets, then tear around the room like small, frosting-covered tornadoes. The idea that sugar causes hyperactivity is one of the most widely held beliefs in parenting — and one of the most thoroughly debunked in nutrition science. Researchers have been testing this claim since the 1970s, and the verdict is consistent: dietary sugar does not make children hyperactive. What does make them bounce off the walls is a combination of excitement, expectation, and the fact that adults are watching for it.

The Origin of the Sugar–Hyperactivity Belief

The idea that sugar causes hyperactivity gained traction in the early 1970s, largely through the work of allergist Benjamin Feingold, who proposed that food additives and salicylates — not sugar specifically — caused behavioural problems in children. His Feingold Diet became enormously popular among parents, and in the cultural transmission of these ideas, sugar got bundled in as a culprit alongside artificial dyes and preservatives. By the 1980s, "sugar high" had entered everyday language as though it were an established medical fact. Parent groups lobbied for warnings on sugary foods, and the belief became self-reinforcing: parents expected hyperactivity after sugar, children ate sweets at inherently exciting events (parties, Halloween, holidays), and the expected behaviour was observed. The scientific community, however, was sceptical from the beginning. Controlled trials began in the 1970s and have continued ever since, none of them supporting the hypothesis.

What Controlled Studies Found

The most comprehensive review of the evidence was a 1995 meta-analysis published in JAMA by Wolraich et al., which pooled data from 23 double-blind, randomised controlled trials. The conclusion was unambiguous: sugar — including sucrose and aspartame — does not affect children's behaviour or cognitive performance, even in children labelled 'sugar-sensitive' by their parents, and even in those with diagnosed ADHD. Individual studies reinforced this finding in particularly elegant ways. In one frequently cited experiment, researchers told parents that their children had consumed a large sugar load. The children had actually consumed a sugar-free placebo. Parents who believed their children had eaten sugar rated their behaviour as significantly more hyperactive than parents told the truth — despite identical behaviour. This is a textbook demonstration of expectation bias: the belief creates the observation, not the biochemistry. Subsequent studies have repeatedly confirmed that neither the glycaemic load nor the specific type of sugar consumed predicts activity levels, attention, or behaviour ratings in controlled conditions.

Why the Myth Persists

The persistence of the sugar–hyperactivity myth despite decades of contrary evidence illustrates how confirmation bias operates in everyday life. Sugar tends to be consumed at events that are inherently stimulating: birthday parties, holiday celebrations, Halloween trick-or-treating. At these events, children are excited, overstimulated, and often sleep-deprived — each of which independently increases activity and reduces impulse control. Adults then attribute the behaviour they observe to the most visible variable — the sweets — rather than to the context. The expectation itself also plays a role. When adults believe children have consumed sugar, they interact with them differently: they may be more watchful, more reactive to minor behaviours, or inadvertently signal their expectations through their own demeanour. Children respond to these cues. This mechanism — where adult expectation shapes adult perception of child behaviour — is precisely what the double-blind study design is meant to eliminate. When neither parent nor child knows whether sugar or placebo was consumed, the hyperactivity effect disappears entirely.

The Verdict

Sugar does not cause hyperactivity in children. This conclusion is supported by more than 23 controlled trials spanning five decades and has been endorsed by the American Academy of Pediatrics and every major nutritional authority. What parents observe at sugar-rich events is explained by the excitement of the occasion, sleep disruption, and expectation bias — not glucose metabolism. Sugar still has genuine health costs: dental caries, excess caloric intake, and long-term metabolic effects are all real concerns supported by solid evidence. But making children bounce off the walls is not among them. The hyperactivity belief is a compelling example of how a culturally embedded narrative can survive overwhelming scientific refutation simply because it confirms what parents already expect to see.

What About ADHD and Sugar Sensitivity?

Parents of children with ADHD are particularly likely to believe that sugar worsens their child's symptoms, and surveys suggest many restrict sugar on this basis. The controlled evidence does not support this. Wolraich's 1995 meta-analysis specifically examined 'sugar-sensitive' children — those identified by their parents as especially reactive to sugar — and found no behavioural difference between sugar and placebo conditions. A 1994 double-blind crossover study by Wolraich et al. in the New England Journal of Medicine confirmed the same null result in children with ADHD, using careful dietary control. This does not mean diet is irrelevant to ADHD management. Emerging research on omega-3 fatty acids, iron status, and sleep hygiene suggests genuinely promising directions. But dietary sugar, specifically, has been tested more rigorously than almost any other nutritional hypothesis in paediatric behaviour, and it has failed to demonstrate an effect every time. Restricting sugar is not harmful in itself, and the broader dietary benefits of reduced sugar intake are real — but the mechanism parents assume (glucose driving hyperactivity) is not operative.

Frequently Asked Questions

Does sugar make children hyperactive?

No. More than 23 double-blind controlled trials have found no link between sugar consumption and hyperactivity in children. The behaviour parents observe at sugar-rich events is explained by the excitement of the occasion and adult expectation bias, not glucose metabolism.

Why do children seem hyper after eating sugar?

Sugar tends to be consumed at inherently stimulating events — birthday parties, holidays, Halloween — where children are excited and sleep-deprived. Adults who expect hyperactivity after sugar also observe and rate behaviour differently, a well-documented expectation bias effect.

Does sugar worsen ADHD symptoms?

Controlled trials have not found a connection. The 1994 Wolraich et al. study in the New England Journal of Medicine specifically tested children with ADHD under careful dietary conditions and found no behavioural difference between sugar and sugar-free placebo.

What is the 'sugar high'?

The 'sugar high' is a popular belief that eating large amounts of sugar causes a noticeable spike in energy and activity, followed by a crash. Neither the initial spike nor the subsequent crash has been reliably demonstrated in controlled research. Blood glucose changes from normal sugar consumption are too modest and gradual to produce these effects.

Is sugar harmful to children despite not causing hyperactivity?

Yes, but for different reasons. High sugar intake is associated with dental caries, excess caloric intake, and long-term metabolic effects including obesity and type 2 diabetes risk. These are genuine evidence-based concerns — the hyperactivity claim simply isn't one of them.

How was the sugar-hyperactivity myth studied?

Researchers used double-blind, placebo-controlled designs where neither parents nor children knew whether sugar or a sugar-free substitute had been consumed. In these conditions, sugar consistently produced no measurable difference in activity levels, attention, or behaviour ratings.

Did Benjamin Feingold claim sugar causes hyperactivity?

Not specifically. Feingold's 1970s work focused on artificial food additives and salicylates, not sugar. Sugar became associated with hyperactivity through cultural transmission of these ideas, where the public conflated multiple dietary concerns into a general 'bad food = hyper kids' narrative.
