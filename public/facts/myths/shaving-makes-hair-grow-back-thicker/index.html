Does Shaving Make Hair Grow Back Thicker? The Dermatology Answer

By SmartAss Facts Team · Last updated 2026-05-23 · More myths debunked →

⚡ Quick Answer

False. Shaving has no effect on hair thickness, colour, or growth rate. This has been confirmed by multiple controlled studies and is not disputed in dermatology. The perception of thicker regrowth is an optical illusion: a shaved hair has a blunt tip that looks and feels coarser than the naturally tapered tip of an unshaved hair.

🔑 Key Takeaway

"Shaving Makes Hair Grow Back Thicker and Darker" is a myth. The scientific consensus is clear, the primary sources are documented above, and the exact origin of the false belief can be traced. Read on for the full evidence.

✗ The Myth

"Shaving Makes Hair Grow Back Thicker and Darker"

✓ The Reality

False.

The warning is delivered early: don't shave your legs (or arms, or face) because the hair will grow back thicker and darker. Generations of parents, older siblings, and beauty magazines have repeated this with complete certainty. It is a myth. Dermatologists and researchers have known for nearly a century that shaving has no biological effect on hair shaft diameter, pigmentation, or the rate at which hair grows. The compelling sensation of stubble — sharp, coarse, noticeably different from the original hair — is real, but its cause is optical geometry, not changed biology.

The Classic Studies

The shaving myth was formally tested as early as 1928, when Mildred Trotter published a study in the American Journal of Physical Anthropology showing that shaving had no effect on hair shaft diameter or colour. Over the following decades, multiple controlled studies replicated this finding. Perhaps the most cited is a 1970 study by Lynfield and Macwilliams published in the Journal of Investigative Dermatology, which measured hair thickness before shaving and at regular intervals after, using caliper measurements and microscopy. They found no significant increase in shaft diameter at any time point. A larger 2007 randomised controlled trial confirmed the same result in a diverse sample, controlling for hair type, age, sex, and body site. The American Academy of Dermatology explicitly addresses the myth on its public information resources, stating that shaving does not change hair texture, colour, or growth rate. No dermatological study published in a peer-reviewed journal has found otherwise.

Why Stubble Feels and Looks Coarser

Natural, unshaved hair tapers to a fine point at the tip. This taper is the result of normal hair growth: the newest-formed hair shaft is wide at the follicle and narrows as it is worn and exposed over time, leaving a pointed tip that lies flat and feels soft. When hair is shaved at the surface of the skin, the cut creates a blunt, flat tip across the full diameter of the shaft. This blunt tip is identical in width to the widest part of the original hair — because that is exactly what it is. When this blunt-tipped hair emerges through the skin over the next few days, it pokes upward rather than lying flat, and its flat cross-section reflects light differently, appearing darker. When touched, the flat edge catches on skin rather than sliding over it, producing the characteristic scratchy feeling of stubble. None of this represents any change in the hair itself. If you were to measure the new hair precisely and compare it to an unmeasured hair from the same follicle, the shaft diameter would be identical. The coarseness is a physical consequence of the cut geometry, not a biological response to shaving.

What Actually Affects Hair Thickness

Hair characteristics — including thickness, colour, and growth rate — are determined by the hair follicle, which sits deep in the dermis and is completely unaffected by shaving at the skin surface. Follicle size determines shaft diameter. Melanocyte activity in the follicle determines pigmentation. Hormonal signals (particularly androgens like testosterone and DHT) regulate follicle size and the growth cycle. Age-related changes to the follicle account for greying and thinning hair over time. None of these processes are influenced by removing the hair shaft at the surface. Shaving is entirely superficial — it acts on the dead, keratinised portion of the hair above the skin. The follicle has no way of 'knowing' the hair has been cut, because the cut occurs nowhere near the follicle. What does affect hair characteristics are hormonal changes (puberty, pregnancy, androgen therapy), nutritional status, medications, and underlying medical conditions — but not the mechanical removal of hair at the surface.

The Verdict

Shaving does not make hair grow back thicker, darker, or faster. This is one of the most thoroughly and repeatedly tested myths in dermatology, and the result has been consistent since 1928: shaving changes only the geometry of the hair tip, not the hair biology. The perception of coarser, darker regrowth is an optical and tactile illusion caused by the blunt, flat cross-section of a shaved hair compared to the tapered tip of an unshaved hair. People who begin shaving during puberty may observe genuinely coarser, darker hair over time — but this is caused by hormonal changes during puberty, not by the act of shaving. The timing correlation creates a false causal impression that the dermatological record does not support.

Puberty and the Timing Illusion

One reason the myth has proven so durable is its timing. Many people begin shaving during puberty — particularly adolescent boys shaving facial hair and adolescent girls shaving legs and underarms. During this same period, rising androgen levels cause genuine, biologically real changes to hair: it does become darker, coarser, and more abundant. The follicles themselves enlarge and increase pigment output in response to testosterone and DHT. When shaving starts at the same time as these hormonal changes, and the hair genuinely becomes coarser and darker over the following months, the shaving is blamed as the cause. This is a textbook post hoc ergo propter hoc fallacy: because shaving preceded the change in hair, shaving must have caused it. In reality, the changes would have occurred exactly the same way in the absence of any shaving at all. Controlled studies that compare shaved and unshaved hair on the same individual, or that test shaving at times unrelated to puberty, consistently show no effect — confirming that puberty, not shaving, is the driver of the observed changes.

Frequently Asked Questions

Does shaving make hair grow back thicker?

No. Multiple controlled studies since 1928 have confirmed that shaving has no effect on hair shaft diameter. The hair that grows back after shaving is identical in width to the original — it only appears coarser because the blunt, flat cut tip looks and feels different from a natural tapered tip.

Does shaving make hair darker?

No. Hair pigmentation is determined by melanocyte activity in the follicle, deep in the dermis. Shaving at the skin surface cannot affect the follicle. The appearance of darker stubble is an optical effect of the blunt tip reflecting light differently and holding more shadow.

Does shaving make hair grow faster?

No. Hair growth rate is controlled by the follicle growth cycle, which is unrelated to what happens at the surface. Studies measuring hair growth rate before and after shaving find no significant difference.

Why does stubble feel so coarse?

Unshaved hair tapers to a fine point that lies flat against skin. Shaving creates a flat, blunt cross-section across the full diameter of the shaft. This blunt edge catches on skin and clothing rather than sliding over it, producing the scratchy sensation of stubble. The hair itself is unchanged.

My hair seemed to get thicker after I started shaving. Why?

Most people begin shaving during puberty, when rising androgen levels genuinely cause hair to become coarser and darker due to hormonal effects on follicles. The shaving and the hormonal change happen simultaneously, creating a false impression that shaving is the cause.

Who first proved shaving doesn't affect hair growth?

Mildred Trotter published the first systematic study in 1928 in the Anatomical Record, finding no effect of shaving on hair shaft diameter or colour. Multiple studies over the following century confirmed the finding.

Does waxing or laser hair removal affect hair differently than shaving?

Waxing removes hair from the follicle rather than cutting at the skin surface, but it also does not make regrowth coarser — the hair follicle is not permanently changed. Laser hair removal does affect the follicle itself, which can lead to thinner or absent regrowth, but this is the exception rather than the rule for hair removal methods.
