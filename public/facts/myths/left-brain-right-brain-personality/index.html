Are People Left-Brained or Right-Brained? What Neuroscience Says

By SmartAss Facts Team · Last updated 2026-05-23 · More myths debunked →

⚡ Quick Answer

False. The idea that people have a dominant 'left brain' (logical) or 'right brain' (creative) is not supported by neuroscience. Brain imaging studies show that virtually all cognitive tasks activate networks spanning both hemispheres simultaneously. There is no evidence that individuals preferentially use one side over the other.

🔑 Key Takeaway

"People Are Either Left-Brained or Right-Brained" is a myth. The scientific consensus is clear, the primary sources are documented above, and the exact origin of the false belief can be traced. Read on for the full evidence.

✗ The Myth

"People Are Either Left-Brained or Right-Brained"

✓ The Reality

False.

Left-brained people are logical and analytical; right-brained people are creative and intuitive. The framework is so widely accepted that it appears on personality tests, in school curricula, in management training programmes, and on motivational posters. It is also a myth. Neuroscience has been refuting the left-brain/right-brain personality split for decades, and a landmark 2013 analysis of more than 1,000 brain scans found no evidence that people systematically favour one hemisphere over the other. The hemispheres are specialised in some ways — but not along the logical-versus-creative axis that pop psychology insists upon.

Where the Left-Brain/Right-Brain Idea Came From

The left-brain/right-brain framework has a legitimate scientific origin that was subsequently distorted beyond recognition. In the 1960s, neurosurgeon Roger Sperry and his colleagues conducted landmark split-brain experiments on patients who had undergone corpus callosotomy — surgical severing of the corpus callosum, the bundle of nerve fibres connecting the two hemispheres, performed as a treatment for severe epilepsy. In these patients, the two hemispheres could be studied in relative isolation. The experiments revealed genuine functional asymmetries: the left hemisphere was dominant for language processing and sequential reasoning in most right-handed people; the right hemisphere was better at certain spatial and holistic pattern-recognition tasks. Sperry received the Nobel Prize in Physiology or Medicine in 1981 for this work. The distortion came when these specific, carefully bounded findings — about abnormal brains that had been surgically disconnected — were extrapolated into a general personality typology applied to normal people with intact, fully connected brains. In a healthy brain with an intact corpus callosum, the two hemispheres communicate constantly and simultaneously. The idea that a normal person might 'use' one hemisphere more than the other in their daily life has no basis in the split-brain research and was never suggested by Sperry himself.

What Brain Imaging Actually Shows

Modern functional neuroimaging has provided the definitive test of the left-brain/right-brain personality hypothesis — and the hypothesis has failed. A 2013 study by Nielsen et al. published in PLOS ONE analysed resting-state fMRI data from 1,011 people between the ages of 7 and 29. The researchers examined lateralisation — the degree to which each individual showed stronger connectivity in one hemisphere — across 7,266 brain regions. Their finding was clear: while some functions do show consistent lateralisation across the population (language tends to be left-lateralised; attention networks tend to be right-lateralised in most people), individual people did not show a systematic whole-brain preference for one hemisphere. There was no evidence of clusters of individuals with stronger left-hemisphere networks or stronger right-hemisphere networks. The population did not divide into 'left-brain people' and 'right-brain people.' Tasks that the pop-psychology framework labels 'left-brain' (like mathematics) activate distributed bilateral networks. Tasks labelled 'right-brain' (like artistic creativity) similarly engage both hemispheres. Even the functions most clearly lateralised — like language — involve significant right-hemisphere participation in most people.

The Genuine Hemisphere Specialisations

There are real functional asymmetries between the hemispheres, but they do not map onto the personality dichotomy the myth describes. Language processing is left-lateralised in approximately 95 percent of right-handed people and about 70 percent of left-handed people, but this means the left hemisphere is better at certain specific language tasks — not that it handles all 'logical' thinking. The right hemisphere is not the 'creative' side; it is involved in coarse semantic processing, prosody (the emotional tone of speech), and certain spatial tasks, but also contributes to language comprehension and production. Mathematical processing is bilateral. Music perception involves both hemispheres. Visual-spatial tasks depend heavily on the right hemisphere for holistic processing and the left for analytical, detail-oriented processing — exactly the opposite of the popular framework, which would assign analytical processing to the left hemisphere. The actual functional division is far more nuanced than any personality dichotomy can capture and operates at the level of specific neural circuits, not whole-hemisphere dominance.

The Verdict

The left-brain/right-brain personality split is not supported by neuroscience. Brain imaging studies consistently show that cognitive tasks activate networks in both hemispheres simultaneously, and that individuals do not show systematic hemispheric dominance across their brain as a whole. Real lateralisation exists for specific functions, particularly language, but this does not translate into personality types. The myth is a cultural product — a well-intentioned simplification of genuine split-brain research that was stripped of its experimental context and inflated into a general framework for human personality. It has proven commercially durable precisely because it offers a simple, flattering explanation for personality differences. The neuroscience says otherwise.

Why the Myth Is Commercially Durable

The left-brain/right-brain framework has generated an enormous self-help and educational industry. Books, workshops, personality assessments, and teaching strategies are all organised around it. The Left-Brain/Right-Brain test remains one of the most shared online personality quizzes. Management training programmes still teach that left-brained employees prefer structured tasks while right-brained employees need creative freedom. The framework is commercially durable because it performs a useful social function: it provides a simple vocabulary for describing and validating personality differences. 'I'm a right-brain creative' explains — and excuses — disorganisation. 'I'm a left-brain thinker' explains — and valorises — analytical caution. These self-descriptions feel accurate because personality differences are real, even if the hemisphere framework that supposedly explains them is not. The danger is when the myth is used to track students into learning styles, make hiring decisions, or limit people's self-conception of their abilities. A student told they are right-brained may believe they cannot be good at mathematics — not because of any brain architecture, but because they were handed a false categorical framework early enough for it to shape their identity and choices.

Frequently Asked Questions

Is the left-brain/right-brain personality split real?

No. A 2013 fMRI study of over 1,000 people found no evidence that individuals systematically use one hemisphere more than the other. Both hemispheres are active in virtually all cognitive tasks, and people do not cluster into 'left-brain' or 'right-brain' types.

Where did the left-brain/right-brain idea come from?

From split-brain research by Roger Sperry in the 1960s, which studied patients whose corpus callosum had been surgically severed. These findings about disconnected brains were inappropriately extrapolated into a personality framework for normal people with intact, connected brains.

Are there any real differences between the brain hemispheres?

Yes — real but narrow. Language is left-lateralised in most people; some spatial tasks favour the right hemisphere. But these are circuit-level specialisations, not whole-hemisphere personalities. There is no evidence for 'logical left' vs 'creative right' as general personality categories.

Can a brain scan tell if I'm left-brained or right-brained?

No. Brain scans show both hemispheres actively engaged in virtually all tasks. The 2013 Nielsen et al. study specifically looked for hemispheric dominance patterns across the whole brain and found none.

Does being left-handed mean you are more right-brained?

Partially misleading. Left-handedness is associated with a higher rate of right-hemisphere language dominance, but this applies to a specific function (language), not to whole-brain personality. Left-handed people do not think more 'creatively' or less 'logically' than right-handed people.

Are left-brain/right-brain learning styles valid?

No. Learning styles theories, including left/right brain versions, have been repeatedly tested and found to lack empirical support. Matching teaching methods to supposed brain dominance does not improve learning outcomes. The American Psychological Association considers learning styles frameworks unsupported.

What did Roger Sperry actually discover?

Sperry discovered specific functional asymmetries in patients whose two brain hemispheres had been surgically disconnected — primarily that language processing is typically left-lateralised. His work earned the 1981 Nobel Prize but never claimed to support a personality typology for people with normal, connected brains.
