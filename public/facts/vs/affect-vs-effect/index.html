Affect vs Effect: The Definitive Guide to Getting It Right

By SmartAss Facts Team · Last updated 2026-05-23 · More comparisons →

⚡ Quick Answer

In most cases: affect is a verb (the action), effect is a noun (the result). 'Stress affects sleep.' 'The effect of stress on sleep is significant.' Memory trick: RAVEN — Remember Affect Verb Effect Noun. Exceptions exist: 'effect' can be a verb meaning 'to bring about a change' ('effect a solution'); 'affect' is a psychology noun meaning emotional state.

Affect and effect are among the most frequently confused words in written English — confusing even for fluent speakers and writers. The core rule is simple: affect is usually a verb, effect is usually a noun. But both words have secondary uses as the opposite part of speech, which is where most confusion arises. Master the basic rule and the two exceptions and you'll rarely get it wrong again.

Key Differences at a Glance

The Basic Rule: RAVEN

The most reliable mnemonic for affect vs effect is RAVEN: Remember Affect Verb, Effect Noun. In the vast majority of sentences, this is all you need. Affect (verb): 'The medication affects blood pressure.' 'How will this decision affect us?' 'Her speech affected the audience deeply.' Effect (noun): 'The medication had a noticeable effect.' 'What effect will this have?' 'The effects of the speech were lasting.' A quick test: if you can replace the word with 'influence' (verb), use affect. If you can replace it with 'result' or 'consequence' (noun), use effect. 'The cold influences the crops' → affect. 'The result of the cold was severe' → effect.

The Two Exceptions

Effect as a verb: 'to effect' means to bring about, to cause, or to accomplish a significant change. It's a formal and relatively rare construction. 'The new management team effected a complete turnaround.' 'She hoped to effect real change in the organisation.' This is different from 'affect change' (to influence change, which is already happening). 'Effect change' = bring change into existence. Affect as a noun (psychology): In clinical psychology and psychiatry, 'affect' is a technical noun meaning the outward expression of emotion or mood. 'The patient displayed flat affect.' 'Blunted affect was noted on examination.' Outside clinical contexts, this use is rarely encountered.

Why These Words Are So Often Confused

The homophone problem doesn't apply here — affect and effect are pronounced differently (affect: uh-FECT; effect: ee-FECT or ih-FECT). The confusion is purely written. The core problem is that both words exist in both parts of speech, even if primarily in one role each. Once a writer learns that there are exceptions to the verb/noun rule, they can become uncertain which role applies in any given sentence. A secondary problem is that both words appear in similar contexts — wherever causes and consequences are discussed — making the correct choice feel less intuitive. The RAVEN mnemonic, combined with memorising the two secondary uses, resolves nearly all uncertainty.

Frequently Asked Questions

Is it 'affect' or 'effect'?

Mostly: affect = verb (to influence); effect = noun (the result). 'Stress affects sleep' (verb). 'The effect of stress on sleep is well documented' (noun). Remember RAVEN: Affect Verb, Effect Noun.

What does 'to effect change' mean?

To bring about change, to cause change to occur. It's a formal construction where 'effect' is used as a verb. Different from 'to affect change,' which means to influence an existing process of change.

What is 'affect' as a noun in psychology?

In clinical psychology, 'affect' is a noun meaning the outward expression of emotion or mood — the visible, behavioural component of emotional state. 'Flat affect' describes reduced or absent emotional expression.

How do affect and effect sound different?

Affect is typically stressed on the second syllable: 'uh-FECT' (verb) or 'AFF-ect' (psychology noun). Effect is 'ee-FECT' or 'ih-FECT'. They rhyme and sound similar, but are not homophones.

What does 'side effects' mean?

'Side effects' uses effect as a noun (the effects on the side of the main intended action). Always 'side effects' — never 'side affects'.

Can 'effect' be plural?

Yes — 'the effects of the drug were well documented.' Effect as a noun has a normal plural. Effect as a verb is conjugated normally: effects, effected, effecting.

What about 'affectation'?

Affectation (noun) means a mannerism or behaviour adopted artificially to impress others. It comes from 'affect' in its historical sense of emotional performance or display, not from the standard modern verb use.
