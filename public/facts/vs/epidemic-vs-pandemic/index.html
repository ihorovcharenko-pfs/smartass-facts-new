Epidemic vs Pandemic: Scale Is the Difference

By SmartAss Facts Team · Last updated 2026-05-25 · More comparisons →

⚡ Quick Answer

An epidemic is a rapid increase in cases of a disease above what is normally expected in a specific community or region. A pandemic is an epidemic that has spread across multiple countries or continents, affecting a large portion of the world's population. All pandemics start as epidemics — the word changes when the geographic spread becomes global. COVID-19 was declared a pandemic in March 2020 by the WHO.

The 2020 COVID-19 pandemic brought both words into daily use — and revealed how often they were confused. The distinction is primarily geographic scale: the same disease outbreak is an epidemic when it affects a region and a pandemic when it spreads worldwide. But there is more nuance than just geography — the WHO also considers severity, susceptibility of the population, and disruption to social systems when making declarations.

Epidemic: Above-Expected Cases in a Region

An epidemic occurs when cases of a disease exceed the normally expected number in a given location and time period. The threshold is relative: a disease that causes 50 cases per 100,000 people in winter might be "normal" and not an epidemic; if that rate jumps to 500 per 100,000, it is. Epidemics are defined by comparison to baseline rates, not by absolute numbers. They can be caused by infectious diseases (Ebola, cholera, influenza) or by non-infectious conditions — the CDC uses the term epidemic for the opioid overdose crisis in the US, which is not infectious. Epidemics are typically contained to a defined geographic area or community, even if a large one.

Pandemic: A Global Epidemic

A pandemic is an epidemic that has spread across multiple countries or continents and is affecting a large proportion of the world's population. The WHO does not have a rigid numerical definition — the term involves epidemiological judgment about global spread, severity, and the vulnerability of the unimmunised population. COVID-19 was declared a pandemic on 11 March 2020, after spreading to more than 110 countries within a matter of weeks. The 1918 influenza pandemic (Spanish Flu) infected an estimated 500 million people — about one-third of the world's population — and killed 20–50 million. HIV/AIDS has been classified as an ongoing pandemic since the 1980s.

Endemic: The Third State

Between epidemic and pandemic, and conceptually distinct, is endemic. An endemic disease is one with a constant, predictable presence in a specific population or geographic area. Malaria is endemic in sub-Saharan Africa — it is always there at known background levels. Seasonal influenza is endemic globally — it circulates at predictable levels each winter. As pandemics wind down (through vaccination, immunity, or the virus evolving to be less severe), they may transition from pandemic to endemic — still present, but no longer spreading in waves above expected levels. COVID-19's future status was widely discussed in these terms from 2021 onward.

Frequently Asked Questions

Who decides when a pandemic is declared?

The World Health Organization (WHO) is the international body with authority to declare a Public Health Emergency of International Concern (PHEIC) and a pandemic. Individual governments may also make national-level declarations that trigger emergency powers. The WHO's declaration is advisory — it does not compel government action — but it signals globally coordinated response.

What is the difference between pandemic and endemic?

A pandemic is rapidly spreading globally with high and rising case counts. An endemic disease is stably present in a population at roughly consistent levels. As a pandemic matures and becomes predictable — no longer spreading in dramatic waves — it may be reclassified as endemic. This transition is partly epidemiological, partly political.

Was the Black Death an epidemic or pandemic?

The Black Death (1346–1353) is classified as a pandemic — it spread across Asia, the Middle East, North Africa, and Europe, killing an estimated 30–60% of Europe's population. Individual city or regional outbreaks within it were epidemics; the overall event, given its continental reach, was a pandemic.
