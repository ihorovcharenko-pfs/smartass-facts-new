Reptile vs Amphibian: How to Tell Them Apart

By SmartAss Facts Team · Last updated 2026-05-25 · More comparisons →

⚡ Quick Answer

Reptiles (snakes, lizards, turtles, crocodiles) have dry, scaly skin and lay eggs with hard or leathery shells on land. Amphibians (frogs, salamanders, newts, caecilians) have moist, glandular skin, must stay near water, lay soft unshelled eggs in water, and typically undergo metamorphosis from an aquatic larval stage to a terrestrial adult. The key splits: skin type, egg type, and relationship to water.

Both reptiles and amphibians are ectotherms (cold-blooded) and have four limbs (or descended from four-limbed ancestors), which leads to frequent confusion — but they represent separate vertebrate classes that diverged hundreds of millions of years ago. Amphibians were the first vertebrates to colonise land, emerging from fish around 370 million years ago. Reptiles descended from amphibians but evolved the amniotic egg — a key innovation that freed them from water dependency — around 310–320 million years ago.

The Amniotic Egg: Why Reptiles Could Leave the Water

The critical evolutionary innovation separating reptiles from amphibians is the amniotic egg. Amphibian eggs are soft and must be kept moist — they dry out in air and cannot be laid on land. Reptile eggs have a waterproof shell (leathery or hard depending on species) and contain the amnion, chorion, and allantois membranes that protect and support the developing embryo in a self-contained aquatic environment. This "private pond" inside the egg freed reptiles from requiring standing water for reproduction and allowed them to colonise dry terrestrial environments far from water. Mammals, including humans, also have amniotic eggs (the fetal membranes are homologous) — which is why mammals, reptiles, and birds are collectively called amniotes.

Amphibian Skin: Breathing Through Your Body

Amphibian skin is thin, moist, and highly permeable — it absorbs oxygen and water directly from the environment. Many amphibians rely on cutaneous respiration (skin breathing) for a significant portion of their oxygen, especially in cold water. This remarkable adaptation comes with a cost: amphibians must remain moist and are highly sensitive to environmental pollutants, changes in pH, and UV radiation, since all of these penetrate their permeable skin. This sensitivity makes amphibians important environmental indicators — global amphibian population declines are considered a significant warning sign of ecosystem stress. Reptile skin, covered in scales derived from beta-keratin, is largely waterproof and provides a protective barrier, enabling survival in arid environments.

Metamorphosis and Larval Stages

Most amphibians undergo metamorphosis: they hatch as aquatic larvae (tadpoles in frogs, efts in salamanders) that breathe through gills, then transform into air-breathing terrestrial adults. The frog's transition from gill-breathing, legless, water-living tadpole to air-breathing, four-legged, land-dwelling adult involves extensive hormonal and morphological transformation. Reptiles undergo no such transition — they hatch as small versions of the adult and immediately begin the same lifestyle. Some reptiles (most vipers, boas, many lizards) give birth to live young (viviparity) rather than laying eggs, but the embryo still develops within an amniotic membrane.

Frequently Asked Questions

Are turtles reptiles or amphibians?

Turtles are reptiles. They have scaly, water-resistant skin, lay eggs on land with leathery shells, and breathe air through lungs (though some species can exchange gases through specialised tissues in winter). Despite spending much of their time in water, turtles are fully reptilian.

Are frogs reptiles or amphibians?

Frogs are amphibians. They have moist, smooth, permeable skin, lay soft jelly-coated eggs in water, and undergo metamorphosis from aquatic tadpoles to terrestrial adults.

What is the difference between a salamander and a lizard?

Salamanders are amphibians: smooth moist skin, no scales, lay eggs in water. Lizards are reptiles: scaly dry skin, lay eggs on land (or give birth to live young). They are in different vertebrate classes and have been on separate evolutionary paths for over 300 million years.
