Asteroid vs Meteor vs Meteorite: What's the Difference?

By SmartAss Facts Team · Last updated 2026-05-23 · More comparisons →

⚡ Quick Answer

It's the same type of object at different stages. An asteroid is a rocky body orbiting the Sun. A meteoroid is a smaller chunk in space. When it enters Earth's atmosphere and burns up, it becomes a meteor (the streak of light, a 'shooting star'). If it survives and hits the ground, what remains is a meteorite.

Asteroid, meteor, meteorite — three words that often get used interchangeably in news coverage, but they describe the same type of rocky material at three different stages of its journey. The Space Stage: Asteroids and Meteoroids

Asteroids are rocky or metallic bodies orbiting the Sun, ranging from a few metres to hundreds of kilometres in diameter. Most are in the asteroid belt between Mars and Jupiter, but near-Earth asteroids cross or approach Earth's orbit. A meteoroid is essentially a small asteroid — typically defined as less than a metre across. There is no sharp physical distinction; it's a size convention. Comets are distinct: they are icy bodies that develop bright tails as they near the Sun, though they can shed material that becomes meteoroids.

The Atmosphere Stage: Meteors

When a meteoroid enters Earth's atmosphere at high speed (typically 11–72 km/s), friction and compression heat the surrounding air to incandescence. The glowing trail of hot gas and ablated rock is a meteor — what we call a 'shooting star.' Most meteors are caused by particles the size of a grain of sand or a pea burning up completely. Meteor showers (Perseids, Leonids, Geminids) occur when Earth passes through debris trails left by comets, producing dozens of meteors per hour. Fireballs or bolides are unusually bright meteors, sometimes producing sonic booms.

The Ground Stage: Meteorites

If a meteoroid is large enough to survive the atmospheric entry without completely burning up, the remnant that reaches the ground is a meteorite. It will typically have a fusion crust — a thin, dark, glassy surface formed by the outer layer melting and resolidifying. Meteorites are scientifically precious: they are samples of the early Solar System, some over 4.5 billion years old. Three main types: stony (most common, including chondrites and achondrites), iron (alloys of iron and nickel), and stony-iron (rare, include the spectacular pallasites with olivine crystals). The largest meteorite ever found is the Hoba meteorite in Namibia, weighing approximately 60 tonnes.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is the difference between a meteor and a meteorite?

A meteor is the streak of light visible in the sky as a space rock burns up in the atmosphere. A meteorite is the fragment that survives and reaches the ground.

What causes a meteor shower?

When Earth passes through a debris trail left by a comet, the thousands of small particles enter the atmosphere and produce a coordinated display of meteors appearing to radiate from a single point (the radiant).

How do you identify a meteorite you've found?

Key signs: fusion crust (dark, glassy exterior), regmaglypts (thumbprint-like depressions), higher density than typical rock, magnetic attraction (for iron-rich meteorites). Many suspected meteorites are 'meteorwrongs' — Earth rocks. Definitive ID requires laboratory analysis.

Are asteroids and comets the same thing?

No. Asteroids are rocky or metallic and mostly dry. Comets are icy bodies that develop glowing tails of gas and dust when near the Sun. Both orbit the Sun but have different compositions and origins.

What is the largest meteorite ever found?

The Hoba meteorite in Namibia, discovered in 1920 and weighing approximately 60 tonnes. It has never been moved and remains at its discovery site.

Could an asteroid hit Earth?

Yes — and it has many times in Earth's history. NASA's Planetary Defense programme tracks near-Earth objects. No known asteroid currently poses a significant threat within the next century, but monitoring continues.

What is a fireball?

A fireball (or bolide) is an exceptionally bright meteor, typically caused by a larger meteoroid. Fireballs brighter than Venus and visible in daylight are called superbolides. Some produce sonic booms and meteorite falls.
