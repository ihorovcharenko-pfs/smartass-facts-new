Wasp vs Bee vs Hornet: How to Tell Them Apart

By SmartAss Facts Team · Last updated 2026-05-25 · More comparisons →

⚡ Quick Answer

Bees (including honeybees and bumblebees) are typically fuzzy, are important pollinators, and are generally less aggressive than wasps. Honeybees die after stinging; bumblebees can sting multiple times. Wasps are sleek, narrow-waisted, and can sting repeatedly — they are predators and scavengers, not pollinators. Hornets are simply large wasps — specifically the genus Vespa. All three are hymenoptera, but bees and wasps/hornets separated evolutionarily about 130 million years ago.

Understanding the differences helps avoid unnecessary fear and unnecessary stings.

Bees: Fuzzy, Vital, Mostly Gentle

Bees belong to the superfamily Apoidea. The most familiar are honeybees (Apis mellifera) and bumblebees (Bombus sp.). Both are important pollinators — honeybees pollinate an estimated one-third of global crop production. Bees have branched, feathery body hairs that trap pollen, which they carry back to the nest. Honeybee workers have a barbed stinger that lodges in human skin — when they pull away, their abdomen is torn open and they die. This is why a single beehive represents many thousands of deaths from stinging. Bumblebees lack the barb and can sting repeatedly. Most bees are docile unless their colony is directly threatened — they do not aggressively seek targets the way yellowjackets do.

Wasps: Sleek, Predatory, Repeat Stingers

Wasps belong to multiple families but the most commonly encountered are yellowjackets (Vespula and Dolichovespula) and paper wasps (Polistes). Their key visual features: smooth, shiny bodies without the fuzzy hair of bees, a distinctive narrow "wasp waist" (petiole between thorax and abdomen), and usually more vivid yellow and black colouring. Wasps are omnivores and opportunistic predators — they eat insects, spiders, and carrion, and are attracted to sweet foods and protein at outdoor gatherings. Unlike bees, wasps have smooth stingers and can sting multiple times. Yellowjackets in particular are aggressive when their ground nests are disturbed — they emit alarm pheromones that recruit more defenders.

Hornets: Large Wasps, Not a Separate Group

Hornets are wasps — specifically members of the genus Vespa. The European hornet (Vespa crabro) is the only true hornet native to Europe; the Asian giant hornet (Vespa mandarinia), sometimes called the "murder hornet," is the world's largest wasp at up to 45mm. Despite their alarming size and more potent venom, hornets are generally less aggressive than yellowjacket wasps — they do not hover around food the way yellowjackets do. Hornet venom contains more acetylcholine than bee or wasp venom, making stings more painful. However, the sheer volume of venom from one sting is larger because hornets are larger — not because the toxin itself is more potent per volume.

Frequently Asked Questions

How can you tell a bee from a wasp?

Bees are fuzzy and more robust-looking; wasps are smooth and have a distinct narrow waist. Bees near flowers are almost certainly bees; a striped insect hovering around your food or drink at a picnic is almost certainly a wasp (yellowjacket). Honeybees are rarely aggressive away from their hive.

Do all bees die after stinging?

Only honeybee workers (Apis mellifera) die after stinging humans. Their barbed stinger gets lodged in skin, and when they pull away, their abdomen is torn. Bumblebees, solitary bees, wasps, and hornets do not have this problem — they can sting multiple times without dying.

Are hornets more dangerous than bees or wasps?

Hornets' stings are more painful and their venom contains different compounds, but they are generally less aggressive than yellowjacket wasps. The Asian giant hornet is the exception — larger swarms and higher venom volume make mass stinging incidents potentially lethal even to non-allergic individuals. For allergy sufferers, any sting from bee, wasp, or hornet can cause anaphylaxis, which is the primary danger.
