Solar System vs Galaxy vs Universe: Scale and Structure

By SmartAss Facts Team · Last updated 2026-05-25 · More comparisons →

⚡ Quick Answer

Scale from smallest to largest: our Solar System is the Sun and everything orbiting it (8 planets, moons, asteroids, comets). The Milky Way Galaxy is the galaxy we live in — about 100,000 light-years across, containing 100–400 billion stars, each potentially with its own solar system. The Universe is everything that exists — all galaxies, all matter, all energy, all space-time — containing an estimated 2 trillion galaxies.

Few concepts illustrate the incomprehensible scale of the cosmos better than the relationship between solar systems, galaxies, and the universe. Each level of this hierarchy is so much larger than the previous that numbers alone barely convey the difference. The solar system is what most people can somewhat picture; the galaxy is a structure so large that even travelling at the speed of light it would take 100,000 years to cross; the universe contains perhaps 2 trillion such structures.

The Solar System: Our Local Neighbourhood

The Solar System consists of the Sun — a medium-sized star — and everything gravitationally bound to it. The eight planets (Mercury, Venus, Earth, Mars, Jupiter, Saturn, Uranus, Neptune) are the most familiar components, but the Solar System also includes 200+ moons, five dwarf planets (including Pluto), the asteroid belt between Mars and Jupiter, the Kuiper Belt (a region of icy bodies beyond Neptune), and the distant hypothetical Oort Cloud — a sphere of comets that may extend up to 2 light-years from the Sun. The Solar System formed ~4.6 billion years ago from a collapsing cloud of gas and dust.

The Galaxy: A City of Stars

Our Solar System sits in the Milky Way Galaxy — a barred spiral galaxy roughly 100,000 light-years across and 1,000 light-years thick at the disc. The Milky Way contains 100–400 billion stars, and most of them likely host planetary systems — meaning there may be hundreds of billions of solar systems in our galaxy alone. The Solar System is located in the Orion Arm, about 26,000 light-years from the galactic centre. At the centre of the Milky Way is a supermassive black hole, Sagittarius A*, with a mass about 4 million times that of our Sun. The Milky Way is part of the Local Group — a cluster of about 50 galaxies that includes the Andromeda Galaxy.

The Universe: Everything, Everywhere

The Universe is the totality of all space, time, matter, and energy that exists. The observable universe — the portion we can see, limited by how far light has had time to travel since the Big Bang 13.8 billion years ago — has a radius of about 46 billion light-years (larger than 13.8 billion light-years because space has been expanding). It contains an estimated 2 trillion galaxies. The total universe may be much larger than the observable portion — possibly infinite. Beyond the observable universe, physics as currently understood cannot make predictions. Dark matter (about 27% of the universe's energy-mass content) and dark energy (about 68%) are major components that are not well understood.

Frequently Asked Questions

How many solar systems are in the Milky Way?

Estimates vary widely, but most astronomers believe the Milky Way contains hundreds of billions of planetary systems. As of 2024, over 5,600 exoplanets have been confirmed in our galaxy, but statistical extrapolation from missions like Kepler suggests most stars (perhaps 70%+) have planets.

Is there anything bigger than the universe?

By definition, the universe contains everything. Some theoretical models propose a "multiverse" — a collection of multiple separate universes, each with their own physical constants and histories. This remains speculative and is not currently empirically testable.

What is the difference between the Milky Way and the universe?

The Milky Way is our home galaxy — one of an estimated 2 trillion galaxies in the observable universe. The universe contains all galaxies, including the Milky Way. The Milky Way has a diameter of about 100,000 light-years; the observable universe is about 93 billion light-years across.
