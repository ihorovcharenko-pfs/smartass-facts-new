Fruit vs Vegetable: Botanical vs Culinary Definitions

By SmartAss Facts Team · Last updated 2026-05-25 · More comparisons →

⚡ Quick Answer

The distinction depends on whether you're using the botanical or culinary definition. Botanically, a fruit is the mature ovary of a flowering plant, usually containing seeds — so tomatoes, cucumbers, peppers, avocados, and squash are all fruits. Culinarily, a vegetable is any edible plant part used in savoury cooking. The two definitions diverge: tomatoes are botanical fruits but culinary vegetables. Both definitions are correct within their contexts.

Few questions provoke more debate at dinner tables than "Is a tomato a fruit or vegetable?" The confusion stems from two legitimate but different classification systems operating simultaneously. Botanists define fruit structurally: it's the part of a plant that contains seeds and develops from the flower's ovary. Chefs define vegetables functionally: they're savoury plant parts used in cooking. Many foods are fruits under one system and vegetables under the other — and both classifications are simultaneously correct. The US Supreme Court even weighed in — ruling in 1893 that tomatoes were vegetables for tariff purposes, without any pretence that they were botanically vegetables.

The Botanical Definition of Fruit

In botany, a fruit is the mature ovary of a flowering plant (angiosperm), typically enclosing seeds. It develops from the fertilised flower after pollination. The biological purpose of fruit is seed dispersal: the fruit protects the seeds and attracts animals to eat them (and disperse the seeds elsewhere). By this definition, tomatoes, peppers, cucumbers, squash, avocados, aubergines/eggplants, peas in their pods, and even nuts (technically dry fruits) are all fruits. Strawberries are botanically "accessory fruits" because the fleshy part develops not from the ovary but from the receptacle. Bananas are technically berries; strawberries technically aren't. Botany is relentlessly unconcerned with our culinary categories.

The Culinary Definition of Vegetable

"Vegetable" is not a scientific term — it is a culinary and everyday one. In the kitchen, a vegetable is broadly any edible part of a plant that is not sweet and is used in savoury cooking. This captures leaves (lettuce, spinach, cabbage), roots (carrots, parsnips, beetroot), stems (celery, asparagus), bulbs (onions, garlic), and tubers (potatoes) — none of which are fruits botanically. It also includes botanical fruits that are eaten in savoury contexts: tomatoes, courgettes, peppers, aubergines. The culinary category is based on culinary usage and flavour profile, not plant anatomy.

Why Both Definitions Are Valid

The two definitions serve different purposes and don't conflict — they operate in different domains. A botanist classifying plant reproductive structures needs the botanical definition; a chef building a recipe needs the culinary one. The confusion arises when people assume there is one "correct" answer. The US Supreme Court case Nix v. Hedden (1893) ruled that tomatoes were vegetables "in the common language of the people, whether sold in the market or used at the dinner table." The court explicitly acknowledged the botanical classification while ruling for the culinary one in a tariff case. Both remain correct in their own contexts.

Frequently Asked Questions

Is a tomato a fruit or vegetable?

Both — depending on which definition you use. Botanically, a tomato is a fruit (it develops from the flower's ovary and contains seeds). Culinarily, it is a vegetable (it is used in savoury cooking). The US Supreme Court ruled it a vegetable for tariff purposes in 1893, without disputing the botanical classification.

Is an avocado a fruit or vegetable?

Botanically, an avocado is a fruit — specifically a large berry with a single large seed. Culinarily, it is used in both savoury (guacamole, salads) and sweet contexts. Most food guides classify it as a fruit or its own category.

What vegetables are technically fruits?

Tomatoes, cucumbers, peppers (capsicums), courgettes/zucchini, aubergines/eggplants, squash, pumpkins, okra, corn kernels, and avocados are all botanical fruits that most people call vegetables. Peas in pods and beans in pods are also technically fruits (the pods are the fruit; the peas/beans are the seeds).
