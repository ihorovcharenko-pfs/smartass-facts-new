Whisky vs Whiskey: Is There Actually a Difference?

By SmartAss Facts Team · Last updated 2026-05-23 · More comparisons →

⚡ Quick Answer

Both spellings refer to the same type of distilled grain spirit, but the spelling varies by country of origin. Scotland, Japan, Canada, and most of the world use 'whisky'. Ireland and the United States typically use 'whiskey'. There is no universal rule about which is correct — both are standard English spellings in their respective traditions.

Whisky or whiskey — which is correct? Both are. The two spellings reflect national and regional traditions rather than a difference in the product itself. Scotch, Japanese, and Canadian distillers write 'whisky.' Irish and most American distillers write 'whiskey.' The variation is rooted in historical branding, national pride, and the desire to distinguish local products from Scottish competitors. Knowing the conventions helps you read labels correctly and avoids embarrassing mistakes in the company of whisky enthusiasts.

The Historical Split

Both spellings derive from the Irish and Scottish Gaelic 'uisce beatha' (water of life). In the late nineteenth century, Irish distillers began distinguishing their product from Scotch whisky — which had gained a poor reputation at the time due to industrial production methods — by adding the 'e': 'whiskey'. Irish immigrants brought this spelling to the United States, where 'whiskey' became standard for domestically produced spirits. Scottish producers retained 'whisky', and this became the standard in Scotland and, by adoption, Japan and Canada. The split is more marketing and national pride than linguistics — both spellings were in use in Britain and Ireland before the distinction hardened.

How the Two Main Styles Differ

While spelling doesn't indicate a fundamental product difference, the major whisky-producing nations do have distinct production methods and flavour profiles. Scotch whisky (must be distilled and matured in Scotland, minimum 3 years in oak): single malt (pot still, 100% malted barley, one distillery) vs. blended (multiple grain and malt whiskies). Smoky peated expressions are distinctively Scottish. Irish whiskey: typically triple-distilled (lighter, smoother), can use unmalted barley (pot still whiskey). Generally lighter and less smoky than Scotch. Bourbon (American): mash bill ≥51% corn, new charred oak barrels, produced in the USA. Sweet, vanilla, caramel character. Japanese whisky: often modelled on Scotch but with distinctive style — typically refined, balanced, precise.

The Exception: Maker's Mark

Maker's Mark, an American bourbon producer from Kentucky, famously uses 'Maker's Mark Whisky' — no 'e'. This is a deliberate nod to the Scottish heritage of the Samuels family who founded the distillery. It is not a spelling error; it is an intentional brand choice. Similarly, some craft American producers use 'whisky' out of preference or stylistic choice. The general American convention is 'whiskey', but it is not legally mandated — US federal alcohol regulations do use 'whiskey' in most category definitions, but individual producers can spell their product differently.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is the difference between whisky and whiskey?

Spelling only — by country of origin. Scotland, Japan, and Canada use 'whisky'. Ireland and the USA use 'whiskey'. Both refer to the same type of distilled grain spirit.

Is Scottish whisky called Scotch?

Yes — Scotch whisky (or simply Scotch) is the legal designation for whisky produced in Scotland under specific regulations: distilled from malted barley or grain, matured in Scotland in oak casks for at least 3 years.

What makes bourbon different from other whiskeys?

Bourbon must be made in the USA from a mash of at least 51% corn, distilled to no more than 160 proof, aged in new charred oak barrels at no more than 125 proof. It produces the characteristic sweet, vanilla, and caramel flavour profile.

Is all bourbon from Kentucky?

No — bourbon can be made anywhere in the USA. About 95% is made in Kentucky, but producers in other states also legally produce bourbon. 'Kentucky Straight Bourbon' specifies Kentucky origin.

What is a single malt whisky?

A whisky (or whiskey) made from 100% malted barley at a single distillery. 'Single' refers to one distillery, not one cask or one batch. Single malts are most associated with Scotland but are also produced in Ireland, Japan, and elsewhere.

What does 'aged 12 years' mean on a whisky label?

The youngest whisky in the bottle was aged for at least 12 years in oak casks. For blended expressions, the age statement refers to the youngest component. Age statements indicate minimum maturation, not average.

Does whisky improve with more age?

Not indefinitely. Spirits continue to interact with the wood, gaining complexity, colour, and vanilla/tannin notes. But after a certain point (which varies by wood type, climate, and spirit character), over-oaking produces harsh, bitter, woody flavours. The sweet spot varies by style.
