Cyclone vs Hurricane vs Typhoon: Same Storm, Different Name

By SmartAss Facts Team · Last updated 2026-05-25 · More comparisons →

⚡ Quick Answer

Cyclones, hurricanes, and typhoons are all tropical cyclones — large rotating storm systems with sustained winds above 119 km/h (74 mph) fuelled by warm ocean water. The name depends only on location: hurricanes form in the Atlantic and northeast Pacific; typhoons form in the northwest Pacific; cyclones form in the South Pacific, Indian Ocean, and Australian region. Same weather system, different regional names.

This is one of geography's tidiest tricks: three different names for the exact same phenomenon, depending only on which ocean basin the storm is spinning in. A tropical cyclone off Florida is a hurricane. The same storm system, if it formed near the Philippines, would be a typhoon. Near Bangladesh, it's a cyclone. Meteorologically, they are identical — rotating systems of thunderstorms fuelled by warm ocean water, spinning counterclockwise in the Northern Hemisphere and clockwise in the Southern Hemisphere.

How Tropical Cyclones Form

All tropical cyclones require the same ingredients: warm ocean water (at least 26–27°C to significant depth), low vertical wind shear (winds at different altitudes not too different in speed or direction), and an initial disturbance to organise the convection. Warm water evaporates into the atmosphere, releasing latent heat as it condenses into clouds and rain. This heat drives powerful updrafts that draw in more warm, moist air from the surface — creating a feedback loop of intensification. The Coriolis effect (from Earth's rotation) causes the inflow to rotate: counterclockwise in the Northern Hemisphere, clockwise in the Southern Hemisphere. At the centre of a fully developed tropical cyclone is the eye — a region of clear skies, light winds, and relatively calm weather, surrounded by the violent eyewall.

The Category Scales

The Saffir-Simpson Hurricane Wind Scale (used for Atlantic and Pacific hurricanes) categorises tropical cyclones on a 1–5 scale based on sustained wind speed. Category 1: 119–153 km/h; Category 2: 154–177 km/h; Category 3 (major): 178–208 km/h; Category 4: 209–251 km/h; Category 5 (catastrophic): above 252 km/h. The Australian Bureau of Meteorology uses a similar Category 1–5 scale for cyclones. The Japan Meteorological Agency classifies typhoons into Tropical Storm, Severe Tropical Storm, Typhoon, and Super Typhoon. The intensity scales differ slightly, but Category 5 / Super Typhoon / Severe Cyclone all represent the most intense storms.

Which Region Gets the Worst Storms?

The Western North Pacific (the typhoon basin) is the most active tropical cyclone basin on Earth and produces the most intense storms. Typhoon Tip (1979) remains the most intense tropical cyclone on record by minimum pressure (870 hPa). The Bay of Bengal and Arabian Sea (cyclone basin) have historically been associated with catastrophic death tolls due to dense coastal populations and shallow, funnel-shaped geography that amplifies storm surges. The Atlantic hurricane season officially runs June 1 to November 30, though most major hurricane activity occurs in August–October. The western Pacific typhoon season has no official start and end — typhoons can form year-round.

Frequently Asked Questions

Are tornadoes the same as hurricanes or typhoons?

No. Tornadoes are completely different phenomena. They are small, intensely rotating columns of air that extend from a thunderstorm to the ground — typically 100–500 metres wide, lasting minutes. Hurricanes are enormous oceanic storm systems 500–1,000 km across lasting days to weeks. Tornadoes can occur within hurricanes and tropical storms, but they are a separate phenomenon.

What is the strongest hurricane or typhoon ever recorded?

By central pressure, Typhoon Tip (1979) holds the record at 870 hPa. By sustained wind speed, Typhoon Haiyan (2013) reached 315 km/h (195 mph) sustained — causing catastrophic destruction in the Philippines. Hurricane Patricia (2015) reached 345 km/h (215 mph) near the Mexican coast.

Why do cyclones spin counterclockwise in the Northern Hemisphere?

Due to the Coriolis effect — a consequence of Earth's rotation. As air flows toward the low pressure at the storm's centre, the Coriolis effect deflects it to the right in the Northern Hemisphere, creating counterclockwise rotation. In the Southern Hemisphere, deflection is to the left, so cyclones spin clockwise. At the equator, there is no Coriolis effect, which is why tropical cyclones cannot form within about 5° of the equator.
