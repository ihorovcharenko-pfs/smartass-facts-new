Tortoise vs Turtle: What's the Difference?

By SmartAss Facts Team · Last updated 2026-05-23 · More comparisons →

⚡ Quick Answer

Tortoises live on land and have dome-shaped shells, column-like legs, and eat primarily plants. Turtles (in North American usage) spend most of their time in water and have streamlined shells and flippers or webbed feet. In British English, 'turtle' refers only to sea turtles; all others are 'terrapins' (freshwater) or 'tortoises' (land). All tortoises are turtles (order Testudines), but not all turtles are tortoises.

Tortoise or turtle? In the US, 'turtle' covers all shelled reptiles; 'tortoise' is a specific land-dwelling subset. In the UK, 'turtle' means only sea turtles; freshwater species are 'terrapins' and land species are 'tortoises.' The confusion is real, the terminology is genuinely different between dialects, and the underlying biology has clear distinctions worth knowing.

Physical Differences

The physical differences reflect adaptations to different environments. Tortoises have high, dome-shaped shells providing protection against land predators and insulation from temperature extremes. Their legs are short, thick, and column-like — designed for supporting weight on land. They retract their heads and legs into the shell for defence. Sea turtles have flattened, hydrodynamic shells and front flippers evolved for powerful swimming. They cannot retract their heads or limbs fully into the shell (unlike tortoises). Freshwater turtles fall between these extremes — flatter than tortoises, with webbed feet rather than true flippers. They typically bask on land and retreat to water when threatened.

Longevity Records

Tortoises are among the longest-lived animals on Earth. Jonathan, a Seychelles giant tortoise (Aldabrachelys gigantea hololissa) living on the island of Saint Helena, is estimated to be approximately 190 years old as of 2026, making him the oldest known living land animal. He was photographed in 1882 and was already adult at that point. Galápagos tortoises routinely live past 100 years in captivity. Sea turtles also live long lives — loggerhead sea turtles can live over 100 years and don't reach sexual maturity until 20–35 years of age. This extremely long juvenile period means sea turtle populations are slow to recover from losses of breeding adults.

Conservation Status

Several tortoise and turtle species face serious conservation threats. All seven species of sea turtle are listed as vulnerable to critically endangered, threatened by plastic ingestion, bycatch in fishing gear, habitat loss, and historical over-harvesting. The Kemp's ridley sea turtle is critically endangered. Many freshwater turtle species are threatened by habitat destruction and the pet trade. Tortoises, particularly island species, were historically hunted to near-extinction by sailors as a food source (they could be kept alive for months on ships). The Galápagos tortoise population has recovered from approximately 3,000 in the 1970s to over 19,000 today through conservation efforts.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is the difference between a tortoise and a turtle?

Tortoises live on land, have dome-shaped shells and stubby legs. Turtles (in American usage) primarily live in water and have flatter shells with flippers or webbed feet. All tortoises are turtles (order Testudines) but not vice versa.

How long do tortoises live?

Very long — most species live 80–100+ years. Jonathan, a Seychelles giant tortoise, is estimated to be approximately 190 years old. Giant tortoises routinely outlive multiple human generations.

Can sea turtles pull their heads into their shells?

No. Sea turtles cannot retract their heads or limbs into their shells — their shell shape and limb structure don't allow it. Tortoises can retract fully. This is one of the visible distinctions between the groups.

Why are sea turtles endangered?

Multiple threats: plastic ingestion, entanglement in fishing gear (bycatch), habitat loss of nesting beaches, climate change (affecting sex ratios — warmer sand produces more females), and historical over-harvesting for meat, eggs, and shells.

What is a terrapin?

In British English, terrapin refers to freshwater or brackish-water turtles. In American English, it often specifically refers to the diamondback terrapin (Malaclemys terrapin) of coastal marshes. The term is less common in North American general usage.

Are tortoises and turtles the same order?

Yes — both are in the order Testudines (also called Chelonia). Tortoises are members of the family Testudinidae within that order. All Testudinidae are Testudines, but Testudines includes many other families.

What is the largest tortoise species?

The Galápagos tortoise (Chelonoidis niger spp.) and the Aldabra giant tortoise (Aldabrachelys gigantea) are the largest, with some individuals reaching 400+ kg. The smallest tortoise is the speckled padloper (Chersobius signatus), native to South Africa, at about 10cm.
